The Japanese Page

This is a collection of commonly recommended resources for learning the Japanese language, annotated with notes about the best way to use them. This page aims to solve questions like 'What is the best way to learn the language?', 'How do I get the most out of X?' and so on.

How hard is it to learn Japanese?

The Foreign Service Institute classifies Japanese as a "Category V" Language, deemed "exceptionally difficult for native English speakers".

It's important to know that this only means it will take more time to learn. Everybody can learn Japanese, but it will take you on order of 2000 hours of continuous study to become proficient.

Japanese is a popular language to learn especially among young people, so you will find many online resorces, apps, communities etc to help you.

What to learn

You will want to start by learning the Kana. After this you can begin learning Grammar, Vocabulary and Kanji concurrently until you are a confident enough in your ability to start reading and listening to simple material.

Learning Strategies

There are many schools of thought when it comes to the 'best' way to learn Japanese. Some of the more popular ideas are:

The textbook jockey relies on textbooks to give learners a straight and clear path through the language. You read textbooks to exhaustion, do all the exercises, and then pick a new textbook afterwards.

The sentence miner eschews exhaustively focusing on textbook exercises and instead recommends building up Anki decks of hundreds or thousands of example sentences. Whenever you come across an interesting sentence, or one that has something you want to learn, you add it to Anki.

Full Immersion fully immerses you in Japanese, listening to Japanese content all the hours of the day, and replaces all english language media (tv shows, movies, music, etc) with Japanese equivalents. They don't use textbooks very much, and they focus on accumilating thousands of sentences in Anki.

Anki Droning downloads pre-made Anki decks such as the Core 6k deck and spends their time reviewing their Anki cards. After they've exhausted those, they will begin reading native content, adding anything new they find to their Anki decks.

Setting up Anki

Anki is a flashcard app that lets you create flashcards about anything you want to remember. Anki will then ask you to review them at the most optimal time. It is a brilliant way make sure that you don't forget anything that you learn; it's the cork that plugs up the leaky bucket that is your brain.

Learning the Kana

Japanese uses two different Kana syllabaries in the written language: Hiragana and Katakana. These are simple sets of ~40 characters that stand in for sounds, like our english alphabet. Learning them should take you a few hours to a few days.

How to use a grammar Textbook

You can use the following approach to get the most out of any Textbook:
  1. Skim read the content once to get an idea of what you'll be learning
  2. Read the content a second time, more deeply. Aim to read a few pages a day
  3. (Optional) Do any exercises in the book
  4. For everything you learn, find an example sentence and bring it into Anki
  5. After you finish the book, keep reviewing your Anki cards so you don't forget anything

It is up to you whether you want to memorise the vocabulary and kanji content in addition to grammar rules.

If you are learning vocabulary/Kanji through the Anki Core decks or WaniKani, then I would ignore the particular vocabulary the book is using. If an example sentence you want to bring into Anki contains vocabulary you don't know, you can swap the words for ones that you do know, if you are confident that your change is correct.

If on the other hand, you are not learning Kanji and Vocabulary by other means, then you can attempt to learn the vocabulary and kanji in the textbook

Kanji Resources

The third component of the Japanese written language is Kanji. You need to learn around 2000 Kanji characters to be considered literate. Since learning the Kanji may take you a year or more, you should learn Kanji alongside all of your other studies.

Conversation & Ouput Practice

There are conflicting ideas on when is the best time to start outputting (writing and conversing in Japanese). Some argue that output should be delayed until you have a significant amount of study already accomplished (around a year) so that you don't accidentally reinforce any bad habits.

Whether you choose to start outputting immediately, or later on, there are many resources that will let you talk to native Japanese people to practice your conversation skills.

  • HelloTalk - social language exchange
  • Lang-8 - find language partners
  • iTalki - find Japanese tutors
